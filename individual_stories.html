Edward Day Cohota
23rd Massachusetts

Found as a half-starved stowaway on the ship named Cohota, Edward Day Cohota worked as a cabin boy before eventually coming to Gloucester, Massachusetts. Cohota fought at the Battle of Drury’s Bluff, miraculously surviving after seven shots pierced his clothes. Later, at the Battle of Cold Harbor, Cohota was shot in the head, but despite his injury, he persevered and saved the life of a heavily injured soldier, dragging him to safety before returning to the front. Though he was a proud American, his citizenship was denied by the Chinese Exclusion Acts.

Ah Yee Way
81st Pennsylvannia

Brought to the United States to be educated as a missionary, Ah Yee was dissatisfied in his adoptive country and wanted to return home to China. At 16 years old, Ah Yee enlisted in the 42nd New York Volunteer Regiment as a corporal. At the battle of Spotsylvania, after all his comrades were killed or wounded, Ah Yee took up the flag, and kept it flying. Later in the war, Ah Yee was captured and sent to the infamous Andersonville prison camp. There, he contracted a disease that ultimately led him to go blind, all in service to a country that never remembered his sacrifice.

Joseph Pierce
14th Connecticut

Originally from Canton, China, it is believed that Joesph was sold into slavery and brought to the United States as a child. He enlisted in the army at age 20. He fought in many notable battles, remaining with the army until the end of the war. During the Battle of Gettysburg, Pierce’s bravery was on full display -- he was one of the first soldiers to volunteer to fight on the front lines. Pierce was promoted to Corporal for his distinguished service, an exceptional distinction that defied the stereotypes Asian Americans faced at the time.

Antonio Dardelle
27th Connecticut

Born in Kwangtung Province, China, Dardelle was brought to Connecticut at age seven. Dardelle enlisted straight out of school. At the Battle of Fredericksburg, Dardelle was severely wounded in his right shoulder. He continued to serve through the rest of the war despite his injury. Dardelle returned home to Connecticut, where he married and fathered three daughters. In 1880, he became a naturalized US citizen. Despite his service, Antonio was denied his veterans’ pension due to lacking birth records.

Hong Neok Wee
15th Pennsylvania

Woo was born in a small town five miles outside of Zhangzhou, China. Woo served as a cabin boy on the USS Susquehanna, led by Commodore Matthew Perry. Woo was adopted by a local doctor in Lancaster, Pennsylvania. He become heavily involved in his local church. Despite the concerns of his friends and family, Woo enlisted in the Union Army because he “felt that the North was right in opposing slavery.” After his enlistment, Woo returned to Lancaster before eventually returning to China.

John Hang
US Navy

Hang was born in Canton, China. He came to the U.S. as a cook on a merchant ship. He enlisted in the Navy, serving as a landsman and cabin steward on the USS North Carolina and Albatross. After being discharged on September 30, 1864, Hang moved to Staten Island, where he started a grocery store. Decades later in 1892, Hang would receive his US citizenship, which would be cruelly revoked just 4 years later by the Chinese Exclusion Acts.

Felix Baldberry
11th Michigan

A native of the Philippines, Baldery joined the crew of a sailing vessel before moving to Michigan to work as a farmhand. He enlisted in December of 1863, and saw some of the fiercest fighting of the war. At the Battle of Missionary Ridge, Balderry charged uphill against a dug in Confederate artillery battery. His regiment succeeded in breaching the Confederate line. Balderry continued to fight in several more major battles until the end of the war.

Antonio Gomez
US Navy

Born Conjee Rustomjee Cohoujee Bey in Lahore (modern Pakistan) Gomez moved to London for his education. In 1860 he traveled to Brooklyn, where he trained to become a minister. However at the outbreak of the Civil War, he enlisted in the US Navy. He served three consecutive enlistments as a landsman and ward room steward on the USS Dacotah, Louisville, Iroquois, North Carolina, and Niagara. After being discharged, he settled in San Francisco and had four children.
